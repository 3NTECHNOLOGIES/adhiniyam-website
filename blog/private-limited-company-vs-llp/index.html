Private Limited Company vs LLP

A practical comparison for founders choosing between Private Limited Company and LLP in India.

7 min readRelated Tool: Invoice GeneratorRelated Service: Company Registration
Quick Answer

A Private Limited Company is generally suitable for startups and businesses planning structured growth, shareholders, directors and funding. An LLP is generally suitable for professional firms, consultants and small service businesses that want limited liability with flexible partner management.

What is a Private Limited Company?

A Private Limited Company is a company structure with shareholders and directors. It is commonly used by startups, agencies, tech businesses and businesses planning funding or long-term scale.

What is an LLP?

An LLP, or Limited Liability Partnership, combines partnership-style flexibility with limited liability. It is commonly used by consultants, professional firms and service businesses.

Ownership Difference

A company has shareholders who own shares. An LLP has partners whose rights, profit sharing and responsibilities are usually defined in the LLP Agreement.

Management Difference

A company is managed by directors. An LLP is managed by partners or designated partners as per the LLP Agreement.

Compliance Difference

A Private Limited Company generally has more compliance than an LLP, including board records, annual filings and company law requirements. LLP compliance may be comparatively simpler, but annual filings and tax compliance still apply.

Funding Difference

A Private Limited Company is usually better suited for equity funding because it has a shareholding structure. LLPs are generally not preferred for venture-style equity investment.

Which One Should You Choose?

  • Choose Private Limited Company for funding, scale, equity and corporate structure.
  • Choose LLP for professional services, partner flexibility and simpler operations.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Choosing a company only for image
  • Choosing LLP only because compliance is lower
  • Ignoring funding plans
  • Not drafting LLP Agreement properly
  • Not planning tax and ownership structure

Practical Example

Two founders building a SaaS product and planning funding may prefer a Private Limited Company. Three consultants starting an advisory firm without funding plans may prefer an LLP.
